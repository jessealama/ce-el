Suppose the theorem holds for n, that is, suppose that, for any formulas φ1, …, φn, ψ, if ⊨ φ1 → … → φn → ψ, then ⊨ □φ1 → … → □φn → □ψ. We show that, for any formulas φ1, …, φn, φn+1, ψ, if ⊨ φ1 → … → φn → φn+1 → ψ, then ⊨ □φ1 → … → □φn → □φn+1 → □ψ. • EXERCISE: Let ℘(ℕ)<ω be the set of finite subsets of ℕ (where "finite" is defined as in the preceding discussion in terms of inductive sets). (a) Prove that ℘(ℕ)<ω is inductive over ℕ. (b) Let E be the set of even numbers and let M be the set ℘(ℕ)<ω ∪ {X ∈ ℘(ℕ) : E ⊆ X}. (So M consists of all the finite subsets of ℕ together with all the subsets of ℕ that contain all the even numbers.) Prove that M is inductive over ℕ. • The restriction imposed on Comprehension in light of Russell's Paradox prevents us from proving the existence of the set ℕ of natural numbers (understood as finite von Neumann ordinals). ZF set theory, therefore, includes an appropriate axiom: • Axiom of Infinity: An inductive set exists. In conjunction with ZF's restricted form of Comprehension, i.e., Separation, it is then possible to prove in ZF the existence of ℕ proper from the axiom of infinity. Specifically, by the axiom of infinity, there is an inductive set A. Then we let ℕ = {x ∈ A : for all S, if S is inductive, then x ∈ S}.
